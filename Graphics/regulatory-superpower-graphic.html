An Unprecedented Digital Regulatory Authority.

Three bills, each reasonable on its own. But stacked together and rushed through Parliament, they hand one unelected body control of the entire Canadian internet.

C-34

Speech

Social-media ban for kids, age verification, AI-chatbot rules, harmful-content duties.

C-36

Privacy

New private-sector privacy rules and moves enforcement off the Privacy Commissioner.

C-22

Surveillance

Up to one-year metadata retention, intercept-ready systems, and new police lookup tools.

The Digital Safety and Data Protection Commission

  • Five full-time Governor-in-Council appointees who write rules, investigate, hold hearings, and levy fines.
  • A commission with unprecedented levels of authority.
Source: Bills C-34, C-36, C-22 (45th Parliament, 1st session); Parliament of Canada LEGISinfo, accessed June 16, 2026

C-34

Safe Social Media Act

Bill C-34 sets safety duties for social platforms and AI chatbots, then leaves dozens of enforcement details to a commission that has not yet been appointed.

No accounts under 16

Platforms must block social accounts for children under 16.
The commission may exempt services with adequate child-safety safeguards.

Age gates on adult content

Services must keep minors off pornographic material.
Verification or estimation may require collecting personal data.

Platforms owe a safety plan

Risk assessments, content duties, and blocking tools are required.
Published safety plans must be filed with the regulator.

Half the law is still blank

About 50 key decisions go to cabinet and the commission.
Thresholds, definitions, and enforcement stay unset.

Source: Bill C-34 (45th Parliament, 1st session); Canadian Heritage backgrounder, June 10, 2026; Michael Geist, June 11, 2026

C-36

Protecting Privacy & Consumer Data Act

Bill C-36 replaces commercial rules in the Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act and moves enforcement from the Privacy Commissioner to the same commission that will police online speech.

Commercial privacy act replaced

A new act governs how firms use personal data.
The old act survives only for electronic-records rules.

Deletion, children, pricing

Canadians may request data disposal in some cases.
Stronger rules for children's data and surveillance pricing.

Fines with teeth

Up to $10 million or 3% of global revenue for violations.
Up to $25 million or 5% for worst offences, plus binding orders.

Privacy Commissioner sidelined

The commissioner keeps federal-government files only.
Private complaints go to cabinet-appointed commissioners.

Source: Bill C-36 (45th Parliament, 1st session); Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada backgrounder, June 15, 2026; Privacy Commissioner statement, June 15, 2026

C-22

Lawful Access Act

Bill C-22 could require core providers to retain customer metadata and maintain intercept-ready systems; a narrower rewrite of lawful-access powers that died in Bill C-2, the Strong Borders Act.

Store metadata for a year

Core providers may have to keep transmission and location data.
Retention could run 12 months for all customers.

Not the message, the trail

Message content, browsing history, and posts are excluded.
What remains still maps who contacted whom, when, and where.

Wiretap-ready by design

Public Safety may order intercept-ready systems.
Non-core firms can be added by order, with commissioner approval.

Better than last bill

Subscriber-info production orders need judicial authorization.
Mandatory bulk metadata retention for innocent users remains.

Source: Bill C-22 (45th Parliament, 1st session); Library of Parliament legislative summary, 2026; Public Safety Canada backgrounder, March 12, 2026

The regulator

Digital Safety and Data Protection Commission

Five full-time Governor-in-Council appointees will enforce online-harms rules and private-sector privacy, with binding orders and revenue-based fines.

Appointed by cabinet

Five full-time Governor-in-Council appointees.
Cabinet picks the chair and the privacy commissioner role.

Speech and data, same desk

Online-speech and private-sector privacy share one commission.
Shared rules cover age assurance, synthetic media, and children.

Investigate, hear, fine

Audits, inspections, hearings, binding orders, and penalties.
Online-harms fines can reach 5% of revenue or $20 million.

Independence traded away

The Privacy Commissioner was an Agent of Parliament.
Private enforcement now sits with cabinet appointees.

Source: Bill C-36, Part 3 (45th Parliament, 1st session); Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada backgrounder, June 15, 2026; Canadian Heritage backgrounder, June 10, 2026
