Scoring a Busy Week in Canadian Policy

Scorecard

Announcements vs. delivery

Big on launch dates and press lines, but thin on evidence that projects are getting built. Concrete delivery signals like permits cleared, timelines met, and outputs counted still rarely anchor the storytelling.

C+

Capital & megaprojects

Large procurement numbers help signal intent, but it’s MPs, regulators, and provinces who decide whether ground actually breaks. Ottawa can unlock capital; shovel-readiness still turns on approvals, labour, and sequencing outside the announcement deck.

B

Workforce

Strong policy signal and a necessary stride. Workers still need to show up and employers need to hire. Grants and seat targets only bite if training converts into journeypersons on sites employers actually staff.

A−

Tax reform & competitiveness

Pausing CPP hikes trims payroll costs a bit, but little actually shown here on wider tax competitiveness. Corporate-rate comparisons with the United States and broader investment-facing reforms remain largely absent from this update.

C

Spending

Deficit beats Budget 2025 expectations, but spending stays high. Interest ~$58.7B in 2026–27 (~1.7% GDP), toward ~$80.7B by 2030–31. Without stronger nominal growth or restraint, debt-service lines will crowd routine priorities in budgets to come.

D

Our take: Tuesday’s update sold a strong narrative but the execution gap remains.
