National Electricity Strategy

This draft strategy and consultation document focuses on jobs, security, practical fuels, and large-scale transmission. Many details remain open.

What Ottawa announced

~2× About double grid delivery by 2050, federal framing

Today's announcement: the Prime Minister opened a national conversation about roughly doubling how much electricity Canada can deliver by 2050, with a companion paper on growing the economy on cleaner power.

130K+ jobs Labour for a doubled grid by 2050

Ottawa frames execution around four pillars: supply and storage at scale, stronger inter-regional links, trades and domestic grid manufacturing, and faster treatment of priority transmission.

7 in 10 Expected households with lowered energy cost by 2050

The federal narrative leads with employment, advanced manufacturing, artificial intelligence, and national security alongside decarbonization.

80% Share of clean/renewable energy as of today

Federal clean-electricity parameters continue to allow gas and offsets while stressing large-scale transmission and stronger interprovincial links.

$10B Doubled federal loan guarantees backing Indigenous equity in major clean electricity projects

The Indigenous loan ceiling doubles to ten billion dollars, existing clean-project tax credits remain in place, and a single Crown hub for Indigenous consultation on major files was proposed.

Not a real strategy, and that is a weakness. Today's announcement is consultation on a draft, not firm law, funding, or timelines. Caveats and model ranges obscure who delivers and slip capital and permits past workable dates for a build this size.

Bottom line

More substantive than past federal electricity rounds from Ottawa.

The directional shift is real. But this consultation document sets an ambition floor and defers most decisions, while leaving 90%+ of the $1 trillion build to ratepayers and provinces it has limited authority over.

National Electricity Strategy

This draft strategy and consultation document focuses on jobs, security, practical fuels, and large-scale transmission. Many details remain open.

What we can celebrate

Where we need more

01 Power

The releases frame a larger grid around jobs, affordability, and security, not climate metrics alone.

Today's package remains largely strategy and consultation rather than statute with defined megawatt milestones.

02 Mix

Gas and practical offsets remain in the federal narrative rather than a single 2035 technology mandate for every region.

No incremental funding appeared beyond existing Canada Infrastructure Bank headroom and previously announced tax measures.

03 Wires

Very large lines and stronger east-west ties are identified as federal priorities where projects have historically stalled.

The implied timeline still reads as measured planning rather than a compressed delivery program with fixed in-service dates.

04 Build

Manufacturing and skilled trades appear alongside new supply, consistent with how major projects are delivered.

There is still no published shelf of shovel-ready sites and designs, and no coordinated single pass across nuclear, impact assessment, and energy reviews.

05 Cash

A Crown Consultation Hub was proposed so major electricity files use one federal consultation window rather than repetitive engagement across departments.

Large multilateral processes add legitimacy and tend to slow firm decisions compared with a narrow cabinet mandate to clear specific bottlenecks.

Bottom line

More substantive than past federal electricity rounds from Ottawa.

The directional shift is real. But this consultation document sets an ambition floor and defers most decisions, while leaving 90%+ of the $1 trillion build to ratepayers and provinces it has limited authority over.
