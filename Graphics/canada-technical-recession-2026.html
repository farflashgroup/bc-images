Technical recession vs full recession

A technical recession is a GDP rule, not a final verdict

The simple rule

A recession is defined as two quarters in a row where the economy shrinks after adjusting for inflation. No committee signs off. The label follows the data.

Caveats

  • Canada often reports each quarter as if it ran for a full year. That pace can turn consecutive weak quarters into a recession label.
  • The rule tracks direction, not depth. After a 1.0% Q4 drop, Q1 2026’s -0.1% still extended the streak.
  • Numbers get revised. A later Statistics Canada update could undo or confirm the two-quarter streak.
  • Headlines say “recession.” Economists often separate a two-quarter GDP rule from a wider, longer slump in jobs and spending.

The unavoidable truth

Three of the last four quarters shrank on that year-style pace. Whether you use the recession label, the economy has been contracting.

Sources: Statistics Canada, May 29, 2026. IMF Finance and Development, recession basics. CBC News, May 29, 2026.

Technical recession vs full recession

Technical label vs a full recession

Technical recession

Full recession

Trigger: Two quarters of lower real GDP, usually annualized.

Trigger: A significant decline across the economy, usually over months.

Scope: GDP only. Not jobs or how wide the slump runs.

Scope: Jobs, incomes, output, and spending weaken together.

Timing: In the GDP print. Can reverse next quarter.

Timing: U.S. NBER dates with a lag, using many monthly series, not GDP alone.

Canada now: Rule met after Q4 -1.0% and Q1 -0.1%. April data may change the read.

Canada now: Investment down five straight quarters. Labour markets have softened alongside weak output.

Key difference

Technical refers to back-to-back GDP declines. Full means broad, lasting weakness across the economy. BMO cited a year of weak GDP as reason to question further rate hikes.
