New CRTC ruling

CRTC rules streamers must spend more on Canadian content

Platforms must set aside

15%

of revenues from Canadian subscribers, for Canadian and Indigenous content.

Why (official quote)

“It’s about levelling the playing field and making sure we can share our stories and see ourselves on screens.”

Pascale St-Onge, Minister of Canadian Heritage, 2023

What’s changing right now?

The CRTC’s May 2026 ruling raised the interim 5% share from 2024 to 15% of Canadian revenue.

Who it hits

Platforms above $25M in Canada

CRTC estimate

$2B in programming support

Sources: National Post and CRTC decision, May 2026. Minister St-Onge, Canadian Heritage, November 14, 2023.

Risks

Court fights, higher bills, legal limbo

The 2024 rule is still tied up in court. In 2024 the CRTC ordered large streamers to put 5% of Canadian revenue into funds for Canadian films, series, and music. Apple, Amazon, and Spotify appealed to the Federal Court of Appeal, and the court froze those bills until it rules.

  • Platforms argue the CRTC cannot require a fixed share of their Canadian revenue.
  • Studio groups say the framework breaks trade treaty rules and singles out foreign streamers.
  • The Motion Picture Association warns the 15% rule triples the cost of operating in Canada.
  • Mandated spending may show up as higher subscription prices or fewer titles in Canada.
  • The CRTC issued a new 15% requirement in May 2026 while that 5% appeal is still pending.
  • Platforms above $100 million in Canadian revenue face added French-language and news spending rules.
Sources: National Post, May 2026. Global News, May 22, 2026.

Reactions

Streamers push back

Motion Picture Association-Canada

“The CRTC’s decision to require online streaming services to pay for local news is a departure from what Parliament intended, exceeds the CRTC’s authority, and does not recognize the nature of the services global streamers provide.”

Spotify (Federal Court of Appeal filing, 2024)

“The contribution requirement amounts to a tax, which the CRTC doesn’t have the authority to impose.”

Apple (Federal Court of Appeal filing, 2024)

“The regulator acted prematurely,” Apple argued, and the CRTC did not consider whether the order was “equitable.” Apple must pay five per cent of Canadian revenue while radio stations pay 0.5 per cent, without the same access to the funds it pays into.

Amazon (Federal Court of Appeal filing, 2024)

“Inequitable because it applies only to foreign online undertakings and only to such undertakings with more than $25 million in annual Canadian broadcasting revenues.”

Netflix (Federal Court of Appeal filing, 2024)

“Imposing on foreign online undertakings a requirement to fund news production is not appropriate in the light of the nature of the services that foreign online undertakings provide.”

Motion Picture Association (May 2026)

“This burdensome framework unfairly targets global streamers with requirements that directly violate Canada’s obligations under the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement.”

Sources: MPA statements, May 2026. Canadian Press and Federal Court of Appeal filings, 2024.
